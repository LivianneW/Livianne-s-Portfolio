Steam Navigation

Redesign

Working with a team of two others, I redesigned the Steam mobile app to fix a navigation system that often felt like a guessing game for users. We replaced Steam's disorganized layout with a hybrid design that pairs a familiar bottom navigation bar for daily tasks with a centralized profile hub for deeper account features like Steam Families. By following standard mobile design rules, we were able to cut down on mental effort and make it much faster for users to find their friends and games.

Problem Statement

The Steam mobile app suffers from a significant disconnect between its information architecture and standard mobile user expectations. It struggles to balance being a storefront, a social platform, and a news hub, resulting in a "guessing game" for users. High-value features are often buried under vague labels like "Community," leading to low discoverability and high cognitive load.

Initial Research & Pain Points

Through our primary user testing, we identified several critical pain points:

  • Low Discoverability: Users consistently made incorrect assumptions about where key features lived, such as looking for "Family Management" in the hamburger menu rather than the profile icon.
  • Poor Information Trail: Unclear labeling, such as "Community," led users down the wrong "breadcrumb trails".
  • Violated Platform Standards: Crucial tools like search filters were hidden within the search bar itself rather than next to it, violating platform standards and confusing users.
  • High Cognitive Load: New users felt overwhelmed by information density, particularly when a lack of profile pictures or clear feedback icons made the interface feel unresponsive.

The Strategy: A/B Testing Two Directions

We developed two distinct prototypes to test which model resonated most with users:

  • Prototype A (The Conventional): A standard bottom navigation bar prioritizing familiarity and one-tap access.
  • Prototype B (The All-In-One): A centralized hub designed to reduce information scattering and consolidate features into a single entry point.

The Turning Point

Our A/B testing revealed a clear "trade-off" between the two designs:

  • The Bottom Bar Win: Prototype A excelled at high-frequency tasks. Users accessed the Store and Library in roughly 6â€“10 seconds.
  • The Hub Menu Win: Prototype B solved the "buried feature" problem. While users often failed to find "Family Management" in Prototype A, they located it in under 10 seconds using the Prototype B hub.
  • User Preference: Interviewees favored Prototype A for its "nicer" layout and lack of subtabs, but appreciated the cleanliness and organization of Prototype B.

The Solution

Ultimately, we chose to combine the strongest elements of both layouts. By merging these concepts, we created a design that allows both approaches to shine:

  • Predictability: High-frequency social and shopping actions (Library, Friends, Store) are instantly accessible via a standard bottom bar to leverage established mental models.
  • Efficiency: Complex account management (Steam Families, Settings) is consolidated into a streamlined profile hub, eliminating the disorganized "guessing game" of the original app.
  • Clarity: We addressed redundancy by renaming ambiguous tags like changing "Community" to "Forums" and rearranging menus from most-used to least-used.
Final Version

Lessons Learned

This project really put into perspective how important it is to distinguish between mobile and desktop experiences; you can't simply move desktop features into a mobile layout and expect a smooth user journey. It showed my team and me that even the smallest details, like renaming an ambiguous "Community" tag to "Forums" can help users more than we realized.

We saw firsthand that when these details are overlooked, it creates a massive "guessing game" for tasks that should be simple. It taught me that even if an issue seems minuscule, it's still important to account for it, because those small points are exactly what cause users to give up entirely.
