OVERVIEW

Designing with, not just for

As part of a three-person design team, I partnered with Itacho Sushi to redesign their website. The restaurant's extensive all-you-can-eat (AYCE) menu and unclear information architecture made it difficult for customers to understand pricing, compare menu tiers, and quickly find dishes that fit their preferences.

Our goal wasn't simply to modernize the interface, we wanted to create a dining experience that reduced cognitive effort while better reflecting the restaurant's personality.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

The Menu That Made Customers Work Too Hard

The experience of browsing Itacho Sushi's website was negatively impacted by a complicated menu structure and unclear information architecture. Customers struggled to distinguish between lunch and dinner offerings, understand three pricing tiers, and locate dishes that fit their dietary preferences. Rather than supporting quick decision-making, the website increased cognitive load and slowed discovery experience.

OUR APPROACH

From "Motivation to Walk" to "Motivation to Move"

From the start, we knew our biggest challenge would be organizing Itacho Sushi's extensive lunch and dinner menus. With so many dishes across three pricing tiers, our priority wasn't adding more interactions, but making the information easier to scan.

Using Hick's Law and Jakob's Law to guide our decisions, we simplified the menu structure and organized content in familiar, intuitive ways. Rather than encouraging users to dig deeper, we focused on improving scanability so they could quickly compare tiers, browse dishes, and make decisions with confidence.

RESEARCH AND PAIN POINTS

User Testing: Validating Our Structure

Because Itacho's original website lacked a clear foundation to build from, we first focused on restructuring the menu experience through a mid-fidelity prototype. Before investing in visual details, we wanted to test whether our new information architecture, navigation patterns, and menu organization supported users' decision-making.

We tested whether users could:

  • 1. Understand Itacho's AYCE offerings
  • 2. Navigate between menu tiers and pricing options
  • 3. Find relevant menu items based on dietary preferences
  • 4. Locate important restaurant information such as address and hours

KEY INSIGHTS AND INTERACTIONS

Pricing and menu tiers needed stronger relationships

Users understood the tier system but expected pricing information and menu options to be more closely connected.

  • Created clearer pathways for comparing AYCE options
  • Improved the relationship between pricing tiers and menu categories
