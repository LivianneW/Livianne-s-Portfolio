OVERVIEW

Designing with, not just for

True inclusion starts when designers stop treating accessibility like a secondary feature and approach it as a means of creating better experiences for everyone. AR gaming has this ability to bring people together and encourage movement, yet disabled players are often left out of this experience. This project was an effort to change that by shifting from designing for a community, to listening, learning, and creating alongside them.

Our team redesigned and extended features in Pikmin Bloom to create a more inclusive and thoughtful experience by introducing accessibility features that support both mobility and dexterity needs.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

Accessibility Barriers in Gameplay

By tying game progression strictly to footsteps with a heavy reliance on fine motor interactions, the current game fails to accommodate the diverse ways people move and interact, closing off a path that should be open to everyone.

THE SOLUTION

From "Motivation to Walk" to "Motivation to Move"

Our team reimagined what it means to go on a "walk" by shifting from a traditional step-counter to an inclusive activity tracker. Grounded in the Accessible Player Experiences framework (APX) , our solution replaces step count with a motion-intensity tracker that measures effort instead. Paired with redesigned dexterity-accessible interaction, this extension ensures that players with mobility and fine motor impairments are able to immerse themselves in the game comfortably and confidently.

RESEARCH AND PAIN POINTS

Our insights

We interviewed four participants with lower-body injuries or mobility-related conditions, including both Pikmin Bloom users and non-users, and identified several key pain points:

  • Effort does not translate to steps: Step counts fail to reflect real physical exertion. Players moving with injuries, slower paces, or assistive devices often work harder for fewer recorded steps, leading to feelings of guilt rather than motivation.
  • People care more about the game than the steps: Users don't care about the steps themselves, rather, they care about gameplay progression (collecting items, completing expeditions, and completing tasks). The sense of momentum and completion is what drives the actual engagement.
  • Movement is bigger than just walking: Users want movement systems to recognize a wider range of activities beyond walking, including strength training, indoor workouts, and adapted exercises.

DESIGN FEATURES

Our Approach

After exploring user flows, we identified two core accessibility features to make gameplay more inclusive for users with mobility and dexterity impairments:

  1. Movement Beyond Steps: Allowing the option to replace step-count progression with distance-based tracking, such as miles, allows users who use wheelchairs or mobility aids to have their movement more accurately reflected. This ensures that effort is measured equitably regardless of walking ability.

  2. Dexterity-Friendly Interactions: Simplifying in-game interactions for users with limited motor control by reducing reliance on precise gestures. Complex actions like swiping and dragging are replaced with more accessible inputs such as single or double taps, making core gameplay (feeding, collecting, and managing Pikmin) easier to perform.

REFLECTION

If this project taught me anything, it's that no matter how hard you try to step into someone else's shoes, it will never replace nor replicate their actual lived experiences. As designers, we can try to anticipate every single use case and roadblock, but user research will always throw you a curveball that completely changes your perspective.

More than anything, this project reminded me why I chose to pursue UX/UI design and why it matters to me. Technology and digital experiences should be accessible to everyone, regardless of one's abilities, backgrounds, or life experiences. True accessibility comes from designing with people, not just for them, by including their voices and experiences throughout the design process.
