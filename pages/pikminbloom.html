OVERVIEW

Designing with, not just for

True inclusion starts when we stop treating accessibility like a secondary feature and start treating it as a fundamental human connection. AR gaming has this ability to bring people together and get us moving, yet disabled players are so often left out of the narrative. This project was an effort to change that by shifting from designing for a community, to listening, learning, and creating alongside them.

Our team redesigned and extended features in Pikmin Bloom to create a more inclusive, thoughtful, and accessible experience by introducing both mobility and dexterity-accessible features.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

The Problem

By tying game progression strictly to footsteps with a heavy reliance on fine motor interactions, current platforms fail to accommodate the diverse ways people move and interact, closing off a path that should be open to everyone.

THE SOLUTION

From "Motivation to Walk" to "Motivation to Move"

Our team reimagined what it means to go on a "walk" by shifting from a traditional step-counter, to an inclusive activity tracker. Grounded in the Accessible Player Experiences (APX) framework, our solution replaces step count with a motion-intensity tracker that measures effort instead. Paired with redesigned, dexterity-accessible interaction, this extension ensures that players with mobility and fine motor impairments are able to immerse themselves in the game with less friction.

RESEARCH AND PAIN POINTS

Our insights

We interviewed four participants with lower-body injuries or mobility-related conditions, including both Pikmin Bloom users and non-users, and identified several key pain points:

  • Step counts don't always reflect real effort: Step counts fail to reflect real physical exertion. Players moving with injuries, slower paces, or assistive devices often work harder for fewer recorded steps, leading to feelings of guilt rather than motivation.
  • People care more about the game than the steps: Users don't care about the steps themselves, rather, they care about gameplay progression (collecting items, completing expeditions, and completing tasks). The sense of momentum and completion is what drives the actual engagement.
  • Movement is bigger than just walking: Users want movement systems to recognize a wider range of activities beyond walking, including strength training, indoor workouts, and adapted exercises.

DESIGN FEATURES

Our insights

After exploring user flows, we identified two core accessibility features to make movement-based gameplay more inclusive for users with mobility and dexterity impairments:

  • Distance-Based Movement Tracking: Replacing step-count progression with distance-based tracking, allows users who use wheelchairs or mobility aids to have their movement accurately reflected. This ensures that effort is measured equitably regardless of walking ability.
  • Dexterity-Friendly Interaction Mode: Simplifying in-game interactions for users with limited motor control by reducing reliance on precise gestures. Complex actions like tapping, swiping, and dragging are replaced with more accessible inputs such as single or double taps, making core gameplay (feeding, collecting, and managing Pikmin) easier to perform.
