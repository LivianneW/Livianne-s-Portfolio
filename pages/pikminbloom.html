OVERVIEW

Designing with, not just for

True inclusion starts when designers stop treating accessibility like a secondary feature and approach it as a means of creating better experiences for everyone. AR gaming has this ability to bring people together and encourage movement, yet players with disabilities are often left out of this experience. This project was an effort to change that by shifting from designing for a community, to listening, learning, and creating alongside them.

Our team redesigned and extended features in Pikmin Bloom to create a more inclusive and thoughtful experience by introducing accessibility features that support both mobility and dexterity needs.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

Barriers to Play

By tying game progression strictly to footsteps with a heavy reliance on fine motor interactions, the current game fails to accommodate the diverse ways people move and interact, closing off a path that should be open to everyone.

THE SOLUTION

From "Motivation to Walk" to "Motivation to Move"

Our team reimagined what it means to go on a "walk" by shifting from a traditional step-counter to an inclusive activity tracker. Grounded in the Accessible Player Experiences framework (APX) , our solution replaces step count with a motion tracker that measures "effort" instead. Paired with redesigned dexterity-accessible interaction, this extension ensures that players with mobility and fine motor impairments are able to better immerse themselves in the game comfortably and confidently.

RESEARCH AND PAIN POINTS

User Insights

We interviewed four participants with lower-body injuries or mobility-related conditions, including both Pikmin Bloom users and non-users, and identified several key pain points:

  • People care more about the game than the steps: Step counts fail to reflect real physical exertion. Players moving with injuries, slower paces, or assistive devices often work harder for fewer recorded steps, leading to feelings of guilt rather than motivation.
  • People care more about the game than the steps: Users don't care about the steps themselves, rather, they care about gameplay progression (collecting items, completing expeditions, and completing tasks). The sense of momentum and completion is what drives the actual engagement.
  • Movement is bigger than just walking: Users want movement systems to recognize a wider range of activities beyond walking, including strength training, indoor workouts, and adapted exercises.

DESIGN FEATURES

Design Opportunities

After exploring user flows, we identified two core accessibility features to make movement-based gameplay more inclusive for users with mobility and dexterity impairments:

  • People care more about the game than the steps: Allowing the option to replace step-count progression with distance-based tracking such as miles, allows users who use wheelchairs or mobility aids to have their movement more accurately reflected. This ensures that effort is measured equitably regardless of walking ability.
  • People care more about the game than the steps: Simplifying in-game interactions for users with limited motor control by reducing reliance on precise gestures like complex actions like tapping, swiping, and dragging are replaced with more accessible inputs such as single or double taps, making core gameplay (feeding, collecting, and managing Pikmin) easier to perform.
